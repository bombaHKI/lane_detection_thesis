\section{Motivation}
\label{sec:motivation}

Maps were originally developed to support human navigation. Early cartographic representations were typically created through manual drawing, where geometric accuracy strongly depended on scale and drawing conventions. In such representations, even a deviation equivalent to the thickness of a pencil line could correspond to an uncertainty of tens of kilometers in the real world.

The digitalization of cartography represented a major step forward, enabling continuous scalability and improved geometric precision. Modern digital maps increasingly rely on satellite imagery and sensor-based measurements. However, these systems still contain measurement uncertainties, and not every map feature is directly observed. As a result, local geometric distortions may still occur.

These maps are primarily designed for general-purpose applications, such as human navigation; location search and Point-of-Interest (POI)-based services; route planning and traffic information systems; tourism; and engineering applications requiring low to medium positional accuracy~\cite{HDMapsSurvey}.

Most of these use cases do not require sub-meter accuracy. For instance, the positional accuracy of OpenStreetMap data is typically on the order of 1–2 meters, although this varies significantly across regions due to its crowdsourced nature~\cite{elashmawy2016osm}~\cite{howGoodIsVolunteeredGeoInf}.

For comparison, a standard highway lane is approximately 3.5 meters wide. Consequently, a typical 1--2 meter mapping error may cover a substantial portion of a lane geometry, which becomes unacceptable in applications requiring lane-level decisions.

In contrast, autonomous vehicles and Advanced Driver-Assistance Systems (ADAS) require significantly higher map accuracy. High Definition (HD) maps aim to provide centimeter-level geometric and semantic information to support vehicle localization and planning. As illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:positional_accuracy}, standard mapping products lack the geometric precision required for autonomous navigation.

HD maps enable a vehicle to rely not only on real-time sensor observations but also on a pre-built, high-precision spatial representation of the environment. This is particularly important in scenarios where sensor perception is limited, such as occlusions caused by large vehicles or adverse weather conditions. Furthermore, relying exclusively on real-time sensor processing is computationally expensive and can introduce latency, potentially creating a bottleneck in navigation systems. By integrating HD maps, the system shifts from a purely reactive, sensor-driven perception approach to a more proactive navigation framework, allowing the vehicle to utilize prior high-fidelity data to augment its understanding of the environment and streamline its decision-making process.

A representative industrial implementation of HD mapping is the Orbis Map system developed by TomTom, a location technology company providing mapping and mobility services for automotive manufacturers and mobility providers. The Orbis Map is a multi-layered high-precision mapping infrastructure composed of heterogeneous data sources with varying cost and accuracy characteristics.

One of its key components is the HD Basemap, which targets centimeter-level representation of lane geometry and road network elements, typically with a geometric error in the range of approximately 10--20 cm ~\cite{tomtom2017hdmap}.

\section{Data Sources and Mapping Pipeline}
\label{sec:datasources}
Within TomTom's internal HD mapping pipeline, the HD Basemap is constructed from multiple data sources that differ significantly in both acquisition cost and geometric accuracy. The two most important data sources used in this process are Mobile Mapping (MoMa) data and Sensor Derived Observations (SDO).

MoMa data is collected in-house by TomTom using dedicated mapping vehicles equipped with high-precision sensors, including LiDAR scanners, panoramic cameras, and inertial measurement units. These vehicles capture highly accurate spatial measurements that enable centimeter-level reconstruction of road geometry. From the acquired LiDAR point clouds, representations such as Linear Referencing Images (LRI) and LiDAR Bird’s Eye Mosaic (LBEM) are generated, supporting precise detection of lane boundaries and road markings.

In contrast, Sensor Derived Observations (SDO) are obtained from external sources, primarily from production vehicles equipped with ADAS systems. These observations are significantly less precise than MoMa measurements at the individual sample level, as they rely mainly on camera-based sensing, visual odometry, and onboard perception systems. However, SDO data is available in extremely large quantities and at substantially lower acquisition cost. Through large-scale aggregation, reliable road geometry and traffic-related features can still be inferred despite the lower precision of individual observations.

Due to its scalability and broad geographic coverage, SDO serves as the primary data source for large-scale HD map construction within TomTom. Most of the HD Basemap is generated from aggregated SDO observations and other low-cost sensing modalities, enabling efficient expansion of map coverage across global road networks. In contrast, MoMa data is considerably more expensive to acquire and therefore cannot be collected at the same scale.

Nevertheless, MoMa remains a critical component of the overall mapping pipeline because of its superior geometric accuracy. In particular, validation and quality assessment processes rely exclusively on MoMa-derived data. Its centimeter-level precision makes it suitable as a reference source for evaluating the positional correctness of HD map geometries.

\section{Quality Assessment and Validation}
\label{sec:quality}

The quality of HD maps is evaluated using several metrics to ensure compliance with stringent accuracy requirements. The most important measures include \textit{Absolute Positional Accuracy} (APA) and \textit{Relative Positional Accuracy} (RPA). APA quantifies the absolute deviation of lane boundary geometry from ground truth, while RPA evaluates the correctness of lane width and relative spatial relationships between adjacent lane boundaries.

During validation, cross-sections are defined orthogonally to the road direction at selected locations. Annotators project these cross-sections onto geo-referenced LBEM images and mark intersection points between the lines and lane boundaries. These annotations may correspond to both physical markings and inferred (virtual) lane boundaries, such as dashed or partially missing markings. Each cross-section therefore yields a set of ground truth points.

The same cross-sections are intersected with lane boundary geometries extracted from the HD map. Comparing these intersections with ground truth allows computation of APA and RPA metrics. Figure~\ref{fig:lbem_cross_section} shows an example of this annotation process.

However, constructing ground truth datasets introduces several challenges. First, LBEM representations are derived from rasterized LiDAR point clouds, which inherently introduce discretization errors and geometric information loss. Second, manual annotation is labor-intensive and expensive. Prior research has shown that even relatively simple interface modifications can significantly improve annotation efficiency. For example, the Extreme Clicking approach achieved up to a fivefold speedup compared to traditional bounding box annotation methods~\cite{papadopoulos2017extreme}.

A particularly important focus area for HD map quality is highway environments, as autonomous driving systems are most likely to operate at high speeds in these scenarios, where precision requirements are most demanding.

\section{Proposed Approach and Contribution}
\label{sec:contribution}

The primary objective of this thesis is to improve the scalability and accuracy of cross-section-based ground truth generation. While current annotation workflows rely on LBEM images to label intersections between cross-sections and lane boundaries, the proposed pipeline directly operates on the underlying LiDAR point clouds from which LBEM is derived. This avoids the additional positional inaccuracies introduced by rasterization.

The company stores LiDAR point clouds from MoMa sessions in a stitched and georeferenced form, partitioned into H3 spatial cells (a hierarchical hexagonal geospatial indexing system developed by Uber for efficient spatial data partitioning ~\cite{uberh3}). However, these aggregated point clouds no longer contain vehicle trajectory information, making trajectory reconstruction a key step in the proposed pipeline.

The proposed pipeline is a geometry- and statistics-based processing framework that reconstructs accurate lane boundary geometries from highway LiDAR point clouds in a vectorized, machine-interpretable format. Each lane boundary is further classified with a semantic attribute (solid or dashed). This significantly accelerates ground truth computation for cross-section-based evaluation and improves scalability of the overall validation process.

The contribution of this thesis can be formulated around the following research questions:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Trajectory reconstruction from stitched point clouds:} How can vehicle trajectories be reconstructed from already stitched LiDAR point cloud sequences, and how does this impact downstream geometric inference? (This includes the development of one baseline and three improved algorithms for trajectory reconstruction.)
  \item \textbf{Lane boundary extraction from LiDAR:} How accurately can lane boundaries be extracted from high-density LiDAR point clouds using intensity-based and geometric features?
  \item \textbf{Robust preprocessing of LiDAR data:} What preprocessing and filtering steps are required to reliably separate road surfaces and lane markings from noise and non-road objects in LiDAR point clouds?
  \item \textbf{Validity of automatically generated ground truth:} To what extent can automatically extracted lane boundary geometries be used as ground truth for positional accuracy evaluation of HD maps?
\end{enumerate}

To address these research questions, this thesis proposes a geometry-based offline processing pipeline for extracting structured lane representations from large-scale mobile LiDAR point clouds. The workflow combines trajectory reconstruction, geometric filtering, road surface segmentation, intensity-based lane marking extraction, and vectorized curve fitting into a unified HD mapping framework.
